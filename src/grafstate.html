Internship @ Grafstate

A post class conversation turned into a once in a lifetime opportunity...

Background

Right before the summer of 2024 I briefly spoke with my professor about creating a mobile implementation of his mathematical visualization software.

Grafstate, created by Dr. William Hollingsworth, is a brilliant solution to his own unique problem.

In his own words:

"Creating state diagrams for lecture slides proved to be difficult and very time consuming for me. I do not see well and have difficulty with point-and-click interfaces. When creating slides for the first lecture on deterministic finite automata, it took me so long to draw one state diagram that it was clear that using presentation software would not be feasible."
-Bill Hollingsworth
Today Grafstate is used in all sections of CSCI-2670 Theory of Computation, a class that is required for all Computer Science majors at the University of Georgia.

Bill enthusiastically agreed to sponsor my internship and explained that Grafstate was just one project under his company "Cynsight." Cynsight's main goal is to find solutions for individuals with visual impairments and low vision.

My Task

Over the summer I collaborated closely with Dr. William Hollingsworth with a goal at enhancing the overall usability of Grafstate in hopes to get other schools using it.

Process

Our workflow consisted of many zoom calls, emails, and mock iterations of features to try to get closer to our goal. Still I successfully implemented three key feature upgrades, including

  1. 1. The ability for users to directly download photos of rendered computational structures.
  2. 2. Integrating Vim and Emacs keybindings into the platform's existing CodeMirror, thus, allowing students and faculty, particularly those with visual impairments, to efficiently write and manipulate complex mathematical expressions within a virtual environment.
  3. 3. A full cost analysis of a certain AWS related feature vs Google's GCP counterpart.

Problems, thought process, and what I learned.

Like most projects, I ran into more than a few bumps along the way. I was way out of my depth working on code that has largely been developed and maintained by one single person over the past 10 years.

Though difficult, this "language barrier" casued me to be more thoughtful about the questions I was asking. Before I reached out for help, I gained confidence in myself to find an answer even if it wasn't the right one.

Through this project, I learned a lot about maintaining a live application, and the attention to detail it takes to run a "one man operation" at scale. Additionally I was able to look at accessibility technology from a lens I had never experienced before.

Unfortunately I cannot share any code as I have signed a NDA.
